              torsteinkrause Mod MarutSingh • 4 months ago

              a, b, c: yes.
              d: you're wrong, very wrong.
              e: your message comes across condescendingly. I assume you didn't mean it that way.
              f: fair enough.

              I don't know you or your experience. Just like you don't know me and mine. I appreciate that you have a different opinion than me. That's fine. That's the way it should be.

              However, don't mistake the simplicity of my solution as a lack of experience. This article is based on 13 years of experience as a paid, full time developer, working in RD/teams from as little as 3 people to as many as 50 people, maintaining many different kind of systems, from one of Europe's biggest portals, one of the world's biggest media online CMSs, the Norwegian government pension system, internet fiber provisioning software and a few more.

              I've mostly programmed in the Java space and have been through an endless range of frameworks, libraries and wire formats and deployment platforms.

              It's with this backdrop that I approached my ageing java application, wanting to add a few more features while using today's tools doing it. I first used the latest JEE stack. Got the vertical up, but spent so much time on the wiring of it all, so much time on the caveats of JPA, JAX-RS, Lombok, unofficial Maven repos, app server tweaks and so on, that I started to look elsewhere. Because it just wasn't fun. And programming, especially in my freetime, should be fun.

              I'm not against Java platform. In fact, I pretty much like it. I spent most of my day in it. Java is wonderful for its conventions and structure. This really pays off when you've got 20 developers on your projects and half of them don't care about anything but fulfilling their JIRA task. Then it's good that we've got a platform that takes care consistency, provides sane conventions and let's you worry less about having the full picture.

              What I wanted with this article was to challenge the perceptions, especially prevalent in the Java world (that I know best), that you have to have a framework for everything (a persistence framework like Hibernate is the best example) and you have to provide 100 levels of indirection and OO layering "or else terrible things will happen" or it looks like you don't know what you're doing.

              I hope you know more where I'm coming from and where I stand.

              If we ever meet in person, let's have a pint or a cup of coffee and continue this discussion :-)

              Cheers,

              -Torstein
